# 14. 30-Day Learning Plan

Goal by Day 30: not connector expert, but dangerous in the useful way — able to ask the right questions, reject obviously bad choices, read datasheets intelligently, and hold your own in a design review. Budget roughly an hour a day.

## Week 1 (Days 1–7) — Fundamentals & anatomy

**Activities:** Read Sections 1–6. Read datasheets for one M12, one D-sub, one Micro-Fit, one 38999. Identify shell, contacts, termination, ratings, mating cycles, tooling. Take apart old cables and find the failure-prone regions.

**Deliverable:** One-page connector anatomy cheat sheet + glossary of 30 terms.

## Week 2 (Days 8–14) — Industrial connectors

**Activities:** Read Section 8. Compare M12 A/B/D/X/L/T coding. Choose connectors for sensors, Ethernet, CAN, and 24 VDC power. Review industrial rectangular / Han-style connectors. Build the molded vs. field-wireable vs. panel-mount table. Do Exercises 1–2.

**Deliverable:** Connector comparison matrix for a rugged industrial control box.

## Week 3 (Days 15–21) — Military / rugged

**Activities:** Read Section 7. Decode several real 38999 part numbers. Identify shell size, insert arrangement, gender, service class, keying. Select a backshell + dust cap for each. Compare 38999 vs. 26482 vs. Micro-D. Do Exercises 3–5.

**Deliverable:** A full 38999 interface definition: connector, mate, contacts, backshell, cap, pinout.

## Week 4 (Days 22–30) — Design & documentation

**Activities:** Design a removable rugged control box. Create a cable drawing and an ICD. Review your selections against every failure mode (ingress, mis-mate, vibration, shielding, access, lead time, tooling). Do Exercises 6–8. Teach Section 7 back to a colleague from memory.

**Deliverable:** Mini design package: selection table, released pinout, cable drawing, ICD, BOM lines, risk notes.

:::tip

After Day 30, the highest-leverage learning is real hardware. Get yourself into any harness design review, connector failure investigation, or first-article inspection that comes through. One real failure analysis is worth five days of reading.

:::
